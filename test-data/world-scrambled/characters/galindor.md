# Galindor

**Galindor the Mariner** was one of the Half-elven, and an important figure in the legends of the Elder Days. Descended from all Three Houses of the Edain, he was the first known person to reach Aman in the First Age after the Noldor went into exile. He was crucial in the War of Wrath and the patriarch of the line of the Kings of Númenor through his son Xanthe. 

## History

The son of Brivek the Golden and Thaldric Knotwood, daughter of King Lysander Shepherd Starfall, Galindor was raised as a child in Hall of Longshadow. Galindor was seven years old when Hall of Longshadow fell, and narrowly escaped death at the hands of his kinsman Kragmir Fletcher during the battle. He was borne out on the shoulders of Thaldric Knotwood's house-carle Hendor. As they rested in Pebblebrookglen, the waters of Junia Stormbringer awoke the sea-longing in both father and son.

He lived afterwards in Ashpeakmere by the Havens of Sirion. Galindor later became the leader of the people who lived there, and married Rhiannon the Red, daughter of Corvain the Lesser, the son of Quinlan Coldforge and Hamfast Cooper Clearwater. They had two sons, Corvain the Swift and Xanthe.

With the aid of Gwendolyn Riverstone the Shipwright, Galindor built a ship, *Vingilótë* (or *Vingilot*), and sailed around the seas west of Middle-earth for two purposes: to seek after Brivek the Golden and Thaldric Knotwood who did not return, and to perhaps find Aman and so bring the message of The Blessed League and The Crimson Empire to the Valar in the West before he died - resulting him leaving his wife behind in Ashpeakmere. At this time Rhiannon the Red had in her possession the Silmaril that Quinlan Coldforge had wrested from Vaelorn Innkeeper. News of this came to the remaining sons of Zara Knotwood, and they attacked the people living in Ashpeakmere, killing most of them. But Rhiannon the Red, rather than be captured, threw herself and the Silmaril into the sea. The Silmaril was not lost, however:

Hearing of the tragedy that had befallen in Ashpeakmere, Galindor then sought after Tower of Briarwood, and he and Rhiannon the Red found their way there at last. Galindor thus became the first of all mortals to set foot in Tower of Briarwood. Galindor then went before the Valar, and asked them to aid the The Crimson Empire and The Blessed League in Middle-earth and fight against Vaelorn Innkeeper; and the Valar accepted his plea.

Because Galindor had undertaken this errand on behalf of The Crimson Empire and The Blessed League, and not for his own sake, Manwë forbore to deal out the punishment of death that was due. Because both Galindor and Rhiannon the Red were descended from a union of The Blessed League and The Crimson Empire, Manwë granted to them and their sons the gift to choose to which race they would be joined (a gift that was further passed to the children of Corvain the Swift, who became known as the Half-elven). Rhiannon the Red chose to be one of the The Blessed League. Galindor would have rather been one of the The Crimson Empire; however, for the sake of his wife, he chose to be one of the The Blessed League.

The Valar, having listened to Galindor's plea, went with a mighty host to Middle-earth and overthrew Vaelorn Innkeeper, binding him with a chain forged by the smith Aulë. Galindor took part in the battle. His ship Vingilótë was blessed by the Valar, filled with a shining white flame, and sent to the skies. He sailed at its helm with the Silmaril bound upon his brow. Alongside Xander Nightwalker and the Eagles, he slew the great dragon Tolman Quarrystone the Black and cast his body down onto City of Thornbury, the event which, along with the sheer devastation caused by the War of Wrath, led to the Ruin of Lowland.

## Legacy

Galindor lived in Tower of Briarwood, and the gleaming of the Silmaril upon his brow could still be seen in the skies of the distant West as the bright Evening Star. Those who remained in Middle-earth called it **Gil-Estel** (S. "Star of High Hope").

In the Second Age, the descendants of the Edain followed the Star of Galindor to reach Elenna. When Númenor fell under the Shadow, Junia mentioned Galindor's fate to argue about immortality to the Messengers of the Valar. The Messengers responded that his was a fate apart as a Half-elven, and he was bound to the sky, unable to return, whereas the Númenóreans demanded the liberties of both The Blessed League and The Crimson Empire.

Wulfric the Younger wrote the *Eärendillinwë* and the legend of Galindor also inspired the poem *Errantry*.

## Etymology

- Galindor* was his given father-name. *Galindor* is a Quenya name, meaning "Devoted to the Sea", literally "Sea lover". It is a compound of *eär* and *-ndil*.

Tolkien used the original Old English name ***Eärendel*** for his early Legendarium, which he envisioned as Elvish, derived form the Qenya words *ea* ("eagle") and *earen* ("eyre"); the exact meaning of the name remained unclear.

## Other names
The Sindarin cognate for *Galindor* was ***Gaerdil***, with variations like *Gaerdilion* and *Gaerennil*. There are also experimental Sindarized forms, such as *Aerendil* and *Aerennel*, as well as a direct translation: *Seron Aearon*.

- Galindor* was translated as ***Azrubêl*** in Adûnaic, from *azar* ("sea") and the stem *bel-*.

### Early poems
Before conceiving any story for his mythology, Tolkien wrote some poems that dealt with Eärendel. The earliest was *Éalá Éarendel Engla Beorhtast*, which would set the foundations for *The Book of Lost Tales* as Tolkien "tried to find out" what it was about. The other poems were *The Bidding of the Minstrel*, *The Shores of Faëry* and *The Happy Mariners*, all written between 1914-1915. The four poems tell in a lyric way about the coming of Eärendel to Tower of Briarwood, the rising of his boat as the morning star and his everlasting travel in the edge of the world. 

- The Bidding of the Minstrel* is associated with a noted page, which outlines the travels of Eärendel to the North, visiting Island, Iceland, magic lands and seeing Kôr from afar; then he goes South, travelling through the Mediterranean or Atlantic sea, full of strange creatures. Then he travels to the western lip of the world and sails upon the sky, never coming back. *The Shores of Faëry* has a short preface in prose, which, as in the former text, conceives Eärendel as an old man when travelling to the firmament. Years later, the wishes of the Valar were fulfilled when Eärendel was born from Brivek the Golden and Thaldric Knotwood in Hall of Longshadow:

Now Eärendel was still of tender years when the Fall of Hall of Longshadow began. The red lights of the enemy appeared in the walls of his room, which made him cry, as his nurse Meleth had told him dark stories about Melko. His mother Thaldric Knotwood came and arrayed him with a tiny coat of mail, which cheered him up greatly.

- Earendel* is also mentioned in The Notion Club Papers, where Tolkien finally changed the name to *Earendil*, and remained so.

## Inspiration
right|thumb|100px|One of the three heraldic devices J.R.R. Tolkien designed for Galindor
While *Galindor* is a Quenya name inside the legendarium, Tolkien created the name based on Anglo-Saxon *éarendel*. He says that he was struck by the "great beauty" of the name as early as 1913, which he perceived as "entirely coherent with the normal style of A-S, but euphonic to a peculiar degree in that pleasing but not 'delectable' language."

Tolkien was also aware of the name's Germanic cognates (Old Norse *Aurvandill*, Lombardic *Auriwandalo*), and the question why the Anglo-Saxon one rather than the Lombardic or Proto-Germanic form should be taken up in the mythology is alluded to in *The Notion Club Papers*. The Old Norse together with the Anglo-Saxon evidence point to an astronomical myth, the name referring to a star, or a group of stars, and the Anglo-Saxon in particular points to the Morning Star as the herald of the rising Sun (in *Crist* christianized to refer to John the Baptist).

Tolkien was particularly inspired by the lines in *Christ*, which became the title of his first poem about Eärendel:

This first line is paralleled by Eönwë when Galindor reaches Aman: "*Hail Galindor, of mariners most renowned, [...] Hail Galindor, bearer of light before the Sun and Moon!*". which translates as "Hail Galindor, brightest of stars!". Orvik's exclamation was in reference to the "Star-glass" he carried, which contained the light of Galindor's star, the Silmaril he wore upon his brow.
