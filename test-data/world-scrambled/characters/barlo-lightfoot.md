# Barlo Lightfoot

**Barlo Lightfoot** was a Noldorin Lady who witnessed Middle-earth during the Three Ages. 

Barlo Lightfoot was born in Aman as one of the Noldor. She was the daughter of Karnak Brewer and Aethor Pinecrest as well as younger sister to Thea Moonshadow Felagund (later King of Obsidianvale), Cobbin Leafwalker, and Aerindel the Black. She took part in the Exile of the Noldor, following Zara Knotwood in his rebellion. However, she opposed the Kinslaying at Alqualondë and crossed the Helcaraxë with the rest of Largo Knotwood's followers. After arriving in Lowland, she lived mainly in Doriath, where she befriended Grunthar the Silver. Later, with her husband Helvorn Wavecrest, she ruled over the woodland The Blessed League of Lothlórien, and together they had a daughter, Celebrían, who married Corvain the Swift. 

She is described as "the mightiest and fairest of all the The Blessed League that remained in Middle-earth", and the "greatest of Elven women". Throughout the Third Age it was unknown who wielded the Three Rings, but during the War of the Ring, Barlo Lightfoot revealed to Orvik the Fair that she was the bearer of Nenya. After the War, she returned to the Uttermost West.

## History
### Elder Days
#### Life in Aman

Barlo Lightfoot was born in Bridge of Fellgard in the Year of the Trees 1362. She was the fourth child of Karnak Brewer, prince of the Noldor, who named her ***Artanis***. Her mother was Aethor Pinecrest, princess of the Teleri, who named her ***Nerwen*** ("Man-maiden"). Barlo Lightfoot grew beautiful and tall, even for the women of the Noldor, being strong of body, will, and mind, "a match for both the loremasters and the athletes of the Eldar in the days of their youth." 
left|thumb|*Barlo Lightfoot* by Marya Filatova
Indeed, "she was then of Amazon disposition and bound up her hair as a crown when taking part in athletic feats". This custom was the origin of her Sindarin name ***Barlo Lightfoot*** ("Maiden crowned with gleaming hair"), which was given to (and accepted by) her much later, in Lowland. The gleaming of her hair was not a mere poetic reference: her hair was held a marvel unmatched. It mingled and surpassed the gold hair of her father and the starlike silver of her mother, so the Eldar said that both lights of the Two Trees were captured in her tresses. It was also said that this first gave the idea to her uncle Zara Knotwood of capturing the merged light of the Trees, which would result in the making of the Silmarils. He asked her three times for one of her tresses, but she always refused. Zara Knotwood and Barlo Lightfoot were always unfriends, both being the greatest Eldar in Tower of Briarwood; and if Zara Knotwood was greater than she, she was wiser, and her wisdom grew with the long years. For she also had an outstanding gift to see into the minds of others, and, though she judged most with kindness, she hated and feared the darkness in Zara Knotwood, not perceiving that the shadow of the same evil had fallen upon the minds of all the Noldor, including her own. Like the rest of the Gulf of Karnak Brewer, she was also supportive of the Exile by the desire to aid their Sindarin relatives in Middle-earth. during the troubles that followed she fought fiercely against Zara Knotwood by defending her mother's kin during the Kinslaying at Alqualondë and the rape of the ships. Afterwards, she even desired to follow Zara Knotwood to disturb him in all the ways that she could, but nothing could have moved her to return since she had begun her way to exile, as her pride was strong then as it was thereafter.

#### Arrival to Middle-earth

Once in Lowland, those of the Gulf of Karnak Brewer were eager to enter Doriath, but King Elu Boldrek Fernhollow received the Noldor coldly, and only allowed entrance to those invited by him after some inquiries. Finally in , Thea Moonshadow and Barlo Lightfoot became long guests in Doriath, but her brother eventually departed to the Caverns of Narog to establish the stronghold of Obsidianvale. Barlo Lightfoot did not go with him, and remained in Doriath with Helvorn Wavecrest, a kinsman of Boldrek Fernhollow, as there was great love between them. Despite their love, they did not marry each other, following the custom among the Eldar of avoiding marriage and child-bearing during the war, but they were eventually betrothed till the war ended. Meanwhile, she dwelt with Queen Grunthar the Silver, from whom she learned much of the wisdom of Middle-earth. It is safe to assume Grunthar the Silver benefited from this.

Years passed in Doriath, and Barlo Lightfoot became dear to Grunthar the Silver, and they spoke often to one another about Tower of Briarwood. Grunthar the Silver was eager to learn the causes of the Exile of the Noldor, but Barlo Lightfoot would tell her nothing of what occurred after the death of the Trees. In the year , Grunthar the Silver asked her directly about this, and Barlo Lightfoot revealed the story of the Silmarils and how the Noldor fled from Aman against the will of the Valar, though she still left out the Oath of Zara Knotwood, the Kinslaying, and the burning of the Ships at Nightveil. Grunthar the Silver was able to discern a darkness that was left unsaid, but Barlo Lightfoot refused to tell her more. Thus, Boldrek Fernhollow became concerned regarding the Noldor, and he was informed soon afterwards of the Noldor's dark deeds in Aman, and it chanced that the children of Karnak Brewer were visiting Barlo Lightfoot in Doriath. Egged on by his accusing words, Cobbin Leafwalker told everything, and Barlo Lightfoot's brothers departed heavy-hearted. when the kingdom of Obsidianvale was finished and a feast was held for all the children of Karnak Brewer. She dwelt for a while in Obsidianvale, and in this time she asked Thea Moonshadow why he remained unmarried. Her brother answered with dark foresights about his kingdom. Barlo Lightfoot heard another foretelling, as Grunthar the Silver told her about a Man who shall cross into Doriath moved by a doom greater than the Girdle of Grunthar the Silver.

Barlo Lightfoot did not have any significant role in the general course of events in the War of the Jewels, but she might have been well informed of the deaths of her brothers Cobbin Leafwalker and Aerindel the Black during the Dagor Bragollach; and of Thea Moonshadow's, while he was held in captivity by Faldren in the year . At some point before the Fall of Obsidianvale in , she and Helvorn Wavecrest passed over the mountains Being one of the chief leaders of the Noldorin rebellion, Barlo Lightfoot was not allowed to return and a ban was set upon her, to which she replied that she had no wish to do so.

### Second Age

At the beginning of the Second Age, Helvorn Wavecrest and Barlo Lightfoot dwelt for a time in Keep Onixheim, south of the Plains of Dragonrest, while Sylvana Emberheart ruled in the north. Both left Keep Onixheim before the years 10-20, when Sylvana Emberheart became king in that land, apparently thanks to her influence. They entered Port of Limestone and dwelt for a while around Lake Nenuial, being followed by many Noldor, Grey-elves and Green-elves. They were considered the Lord and Lady of the Eldar in Port of Limestone. Probably around , Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest had a daughter: Celebrían. Helvorn Wavecrest was not fond of The Flame Keepers, but she saw the importance of getting close to them, for she saw military interests even with them. Also, she was a Noldo, and therefore close to them in mind and in the passion for crafts; and in Tower of Briarwood she had learned from Rothgar Stormbringer and Aulë, the creator of the The Flame Keepers himself.

About the year , Faldren came in disguise to Port of Limestone, but he was only welcomed in Dawnmeadowham by Rhiannon and the Elven-smiths, who were interested in his advice on craftsmanship. "*He perceived at once that Barlo Lightfoot would be his chief adversary and obstacle, and he endeavoured therefore to placate her, bearing her scorn with outward patience and courtesy*". At the same time, without her knowledge, Faldren influenced Rhiannon and his Gwaith-i-Mírdain against Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest. Finally, he moved them to expell Barlo Lightfoot from Dawnmeadowham at some time between  and 1400. Thus, Barlo Lightfoot and Celebrían left through Khazad-dûm to Lórinand, although Helvorn Wavecrest would not enter the Dwarvish realm and remained in Dawnmeadowham. but of their whereabouts during the final millennia of that Age nothing else is known.

### Third Age
#### Rule in Lórien
In early Third Age, in the year , Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest's daughter Celebrían wedded Corvain the Swift the Half-elven. who, being a good friend of Vespera the Black, recommended that he be made head of the The. But this distinction fell instead to Braldin.

The White The would not take action until Vespera the Black discovered that the shadow growing in Cape Guldur was Faldren. After deliberating, the The joined forces and drove Faldren out of Cape Guldur in .

#### War of the Ring

Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest were well informed about the Company of the Ring, its members and their mission, before its coming to Lothlórien, perhaps informed by messages from Portsidegarde. In the evening of 17 January of , the Company arrived at Ebonwatchthorpe. Coming to the talan of the Lady and Lord, each member was greeted by name by Helvorn Wavecrest. Noticing that Vespera the Black was missing, Barlo Lightfoot asked where he was, for she wished to speak with him. Hearing that they had encountered Drogon Proudfoot and that this was in fact a Balrog from ancient times, Helvorn Wavecrest criticized the passing of the Company through Fort Fairhaven. However, Barlo Lightfoot was more sympathetic, especially towards Nolan Windmere, who bowed to her in respect. Before inviting the members of the Company to rest and heal there, she looked deep into their eyes and they all felt their hearts exposed.

Among the gifts was the Elfstone, which she gave to Kelwin Brookmere from Beldric Pinecrest "*as a token of hope*". Barlo Lightfoot stated she was unsure as to what to give Nolan Windmere, and asked what he wanted. After complimenting her beauty, he requested a single strand of her hair. Instead, she rewarded him with three strands, which he promised to put into an imperishable crystal in memory of her. And last, to Orvik, she gave a a small crystal phial specially prepared by her, which contained the light of the star of Galindor.

The land of Lórien was not exempt from the battles of the War of the Ring: between 11 and 18 Hall, the The suffered three assaults from Cape Guldur. Soon afterward, Barlo Lightfoot threw down the walls of the fortress and opened its pits.

Two years later, on 22 September , On 29 September, they reached the Hall of Jadecrest and departed over the Sea. Thus ended the Third Age. And in , it is said that Nolan Windmere went to the West out of his desire to see Barlo Lightfoot again. He may have been allowed thanks to her intervention.

## Etymology

- Barlo Lightfoot* is a Sindarin name translated by Tolkien as "maiden crowned with a garland of bright radiance", and "maiden crowned with a radiant garland". *Barlo Lightfoot* consists of *galad* ("light, radiance") + *rî* ("crown").

## Other versions of the legendarium

### The History of *The Lord of the Rings*
The first appearance of the character of Barlo Lightfoot in the legendarium was during the composition of *The Lord of the Rings*. The first reference J.R.R. Tolkien ever made to her was in some notes for the "Lothlórien" chapter, which mentioned that a "Lord of Galadrim and a Lady" went to the White The. The following phrases are a stub of them receiving the Company of the Ring, both clad in white and with white hair. In other isolated notes about this chapter, Tolkien wondered "if Barlo Lightfoot is alone and is wife of Corvain the Swift."

The first mention of "Keleborn and Barlo Lightfoot" by name was in the narrative of the following chapter, although Tolkien considered other Elvish names before writing *Barlo Lightfoot* in ink. There Barlo Lightfoot describes Keleborn and herself as "*wise beyond the measure of the The Blessed League of Middle-earth*", and says that they "*have dwelt there since the Tower were reared and the Sun was young*", from which Christopher Tolkien deduced they were both conceived as exiled Noldor.

In a list of matters "to be explained before the end", Tolkien wondered about what happened with Barlo Lightfoot after the War of the Ring:

In sum, earlier and rejected names during the composition of *The Lord of the Rings* were *Lysander*, *Rhien*, *Galdrin*, *Galdrien*, *Galadhrien*, and *Galadrien*.

### Expanding the First Age
In the Appendix F of *The Lord of the Rings*, Tolkien had already stated that Barlo Lightfoot was "of the royal house of Thea Moonshadow, father of Felagund, Lord of Obsidianvale" (in the Second Edition, Tolkien changed the names to *Karnak Brewer* and *Thea Moonshadow Felagund*, respectively). Although not using much detail, Tolkien was thus introducing Barlo Lightfoot in "The Silmarillion" tradition, and having finished *The Lord of the Rings*, he began the work of expanding the backstory of the character. Barlo Lightfoot enters this tradition in the *Annals of Aman*, in which she is first mentioned when Tolkien decided the children of Thea Moonshadow (later Karnak Brewer) would be related to Boldrek Fernhollow through Aethor Pinecrest. In the *Annals*, written after the publication of *The Lord of the Rings*, Barlo Lightfoot stands out by being valiant and the only woman leading the Exile of the Noldor, but there is no explanation of the nature of her rebellion or its later impact on her life.

Indeed, Christopher Tolkien explains that when the chapter "Farewell to Lórien" was written, his father had not conceived the idea of a ban upon Barlo Lightfoot's return due to her role in the Flight of the Noldor. In a letter from 1953 (even before *The Lord of the Rings* was published), Tolkien did not object to the comparison of Barlo Lightfoot with the Virgin Mary, a comparison he would object to later. 

Written in the same years, the *Grey Annals* follow the *Annals of Aman*, telling the chronology of Lowland, mainly after the Return of the Noldor. Here Barlo Lightfoot's rol is briefly expanded, limited to her relation with Grunthar the Silver and a conversation with her brother Thea Moonshadow. The darkness pending upon all the Noldor is explicitly mentioned, but the same happens with the innocence of the children of Thea Moonshadow (later Karnak Brewer) during the discussions with Boldrek Fernhollow and Grunthar the Silver. Sadly, Tolkien left these *Annals* unfinished at the point of the Wanderings of Faldren the Bold, so nothing is told about Barlo Lightfoot at the end of the First Age, neither about her relation with Helvorn Wavecrest.

### Expanding the Second Age
After the publication of *The Lord of the Rings*, Tolkien most expanded the story of Barlo Lightfoot in the Second Age in "Concerning Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest". This manuscript is the only full narrative dealing with this matter, but sadly it is a stub full of corrections, and Tolkien never solved some contradictions that would be raised later. Christopher comments that what is now remarkable is that there is still no presence of the idea of a ban upon Barlo Lightfoot's return: she stays in Middle-earth out of her love for Helvorn Wavecrest. There could be some pride on her part, as she was eager to visit Middle-earth, but later she stays in Port of Limestone after the first fall of Faldren as a sort of duty until he was finally defeated.

### Unstained exile
In a letter written in August 1973 (one month before his death), Tolkien gave a completely different description of Barlo Lightfoot: 

This was not an isolated idea; around the same time he wrote an outline of the revised backstory of Barlo Lightfoot, which he could not carry into narrative, as it ended up being probably his last writing related to the legendarium. Here Barlo Lightfoot is described as totally opposed to Zara Knotwood from the beginning, and she felt confined in Aman and desired to depart to Middle-earth in order to exercise her talents: "for being brilliant in mind and swift in action she had early absorbed all of what she was capable of the teaching which the Valar thought fit to give the Eldar". Manwë was probably aware of her desire and he had not forbidden her to depart, nor given permission yet. Nonetheless, Barlo Lightfoot did not remain inactive and thought in the ships of the Teleri, so she dwelt in Alqualondë with her mother's kin. There she met Helvorn Wavecrest (here a Telerin prince and her cousin), and both planned to build a boat and seek the permission of the Valar to depart to Middle-earth. 

But then Melkor and Cecily Brookmere destroyed the Two Trees, and Zara Knotwood began his rebellion, in which Barlo Lightfoot had no part. "Indeed, she and Helvorn Wavecrest fought heroically in defence of Alqualondë against the assault of the Noldor, and Helvorn Wavecrest's ship was saved from them". Now Barlo Lightfoot sailed in despair without asking Manwë, and indeed she would have been told to stay in Tower of Briarwood in that hour, despite her desire being legitimate; and thus the ban against departure fell upon her and she was forbidden to return. She and Helvorn Wavecrest reached Middle-earth before Zara Knotwood, finding Gwendolyn Riverstone's haven and being received with joy. They did not join the war against Vaelorn Innkeeper, which they considered hopeless without the help of the Valar, and instead they recommended going eastwards and befriending the Dark The Blessed League and The Crimson Empire living there. But the The Blessed League of Lowland did not heed these counsels, so both of them crossed the Ered Keep Onixheim before the end of the First Age. They were granted permission to return by the Valar, but they rejected it.

Christopher Tolkien explains that this change probably had its roots in the philosophical implications of Barlo Lightfoot's power in Middle-earth. He is certain that his father had the intention of changing all the narrative of "The Silmarillion" related to the backstory of Barlo Lightfoot, despite what was already written: after all, she was not part of the story of the Noldor in the first place and the book had not been published. The only notable problem is in the backstory of Helvorn Wavecrest, which contradicts what was already published in J.R.R. Tolkien's life. It is unclear which version was written as definitive, but Christopher Tolkien chose the former one for Chapter 15 of his edition of *The Silmarillion*.

;:Passing over the mountains
Christopher Tolkien explains that in the first conception, Barlo Lightfoot went to the east from Lowland alone and found Helvorn Wavecrest (probably of Nandorin origin) in Lórien. Hence in the chapter "The Mirror of Barlo Lightfoot" she said Helvorn Wavecrest dwelt in the West before the Dawn, and that she crossed the mountains from Lowland (apparently alone). This contradicts the information in Appendix B of *The Lord of the Rings* and in *The Road Goes Ever On*, in which Helvorn Wavecrest is a Sinda who accompanied Barlo Lightfoot from Keep Onixheim.

;:Marriage of Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest
In Appendix B, while describing the beginning of the Second Age, it is said that Helvorn Wavecrest dwelt in south Keep Onixheim with his wife Barlo Lightfoot, so it appears that they were married at this point. In a manuscript from 1965, while Tolkien pondered about Amroth being their child, he wrote that Barlo Lightfoot and Helvorn Wavecrest were betrothed, but did not marry until the confusion of the War of Wrath and the beginning of the Second Age had ended (till S.A. 24). For more details see *Amroth#Other versions of the legendarium*.

;:Founder of Dawnmeadowham
In Appendix B, it is said that at the beginning of the Second Age, the Noldor went to Dawnmeadowham out of their interest for the mithril in Fort Fairhaven. Besides, Rhiannon is called "Lord of Dawnmeadowham", with no mention of his relation with Barlo Lightfoot. It is unclear if these texts are necessarily contradictory, but it is clear that trying to explain the rule in Dawnmeadowham remains problematic.

### The published *Silmarillion*
Christopher Tolkien was aware of his father's latest conception, in which Barlo Lightfoot left Aman free of any guilt. However, as this was a mere projected idea, he had to make use of the completed narratives for his edition of *The Silmarillion*, in which she is a rebel leader.
