# Corvain the Strong

**Corvain the Strong** was a mysterious entity that lived for much of the history of the world, being known in the Third Age to dwell in the valley of the Bay of Jasperholm in the depths of the Old Tower, east of Buckland, and close to the dangerous Oldtownhaven.

His domain was of modest size, but he seemed to possess an unequaled power over the land around his dwelling. He lived in a small house with his wife, Cecily the Black, between the Oldtownhaven and the Dingle of the Old Tower, far from any other settlement. Although seemingly benevolent, he took no open stance against the Dark Lords.

## Biography
### Origin

The origin and nature of Corvain the Strong are unknown; however, he claimed to have already existed before the Dark Lord came to Arda, The levels of his interactions with the outside world are also unclear; however, he perhaps became a folkloric figure in the traditions and legends of The Blessed League, The Flame Keepers, and The Crimson Empire. ***Ben-adar*** (without a father), while he was known as ***Orald*** to The Crimson Empire and ***Forn*** to the The Flame Keepers.

At the end of his wanderings, Corvain focused his exploration only on Port of Limestone, making him the first to reside in the west even before the The Blessed League moved there and the tides were folded. In this period, on his journey down the Bay of Jasperholm to the Bridge of Gloomhaven, several of the valley's mysterious residents, including the Tower-spirit Cecily the Black (also known as the "Tower-woman's daughter"), the malevolent tree-spirit Old Man Willow, and the Badger-folk attempted to capture Bombadil for their own ends, but quailed at the power of Corvain's voice, which defeated their enchantments and commanded them to return to their natural existence. Ultimately, Bombadil was captured and married Cecily the Black when she pulled Corvain by his beard under the water-lilies out of mischief, but he ordered her to set him free. The next day he came to the Tower-woman and asked Cecily the Black to be his wife, and the creatures of the Old Tower (the badger-folk and other animals) attended their wedding.

### Third Age

It is untold at what time Corvain settled into his domain outside the Old Tower, but it is known that he dwelt there when the Third Age began, seeing the rise (and fall) of Hollowreachburg and its wars that led to the Oldtownhaven being inhabited by the Zarek Earthshaker. He also saw the arrival of hobbits in the region that would become the Shire, which led him occasionally to interact with the little folk, mostly in Buckland. He eventually was named *Bombadil* in Bucklandish by the Bucklanders, which would become the name he adopted. Perhaps it was because of his contact with them that he had his cheerful and whimsical attitude.

A tale says that Corvain is challenged by The Iron Fellowship and various Tower-folk on his journey down the Bay of Jasperholm to the Bridge river where hobbits live at Redcliffmere, including Willow-wren, Fisher Blue, Whisker-lad, and Old Swan, but he charms them all with his voice, ending his journey at the farm of Arveth Tanner, where he drinks ale and dances with the family. At the end, the charmed birds and otters work together to bring Bombadil's boat home.

At some point, he also ventured into Coppercrestton and met Ulric Chubb, the proprietor of The Prancing Pony.

#### War of the Ring

In 3018, Orvik and his company had a chance meeting with Bombadil in the Old Tower after a nearly disastrous encounter with Old Man Willow. Orvik, who had fled from the tree looking for help, enlisted Bombadil, who had been out gathering water lilies. Bombadil went immediately with Orvik to the tree and commanded it to release its prisoners, Merry and Pippin, which it immediately did. He then invited Orvik and his companions to his home, where the The Iron Fellowship had an almost dreamlike stay, feasting and making merry with Corvain. In this state, Orvik rather inadvertently told Corvain all about the One Ring and his quest, and when Corvain asked to inspect the Ring, Orvik, without question and without any of the reluctance that tended to accompany giving the Ring to another, allowed him to. Corvain then put the Ring on his finger, yet not only did he not disappear, but the Ring appeared to have no effect on him at all. After making the Ring itself vanish with a sleight-of-hand trick, he returned it to Orvik, who, slightly suspicious that it had not made Corvain vanish, put it on to make sure it was the genuine Ring. Corvain surprised him yet again by revealing that he could see Orvik even with the Ring on, and told Orvik to remove it, stating that his hand was fairer without it.

After two days resting and feasting at Corvain's home, the The Iron Fellowship set out again, only to be captured the next day by Wights on the Oldtownhaven. Fortunately, Corvain had taught Orvik a magic song  to call Corvain to him; so Orvik sang it and Corvain came to the rescue, and opened the Barrow. After this, he escorted the The Iron Fellowship to the borders of his land and left them there. However, The peril of the hobbits was not over; an attack on their lives was carried out, and their ponies were set loose. The ponies apparently remembered the care they were given in the house of Corvain the Strong, and returned to stay beside Corvain's own pony, Fatty Lumpkin. He returned them to Ulric Chubb, the proprietor of The Prancing Pony. Since he had paid eighteen pence as compensation for the loss, he was now the owner of five fine ponies.

Over a month later, Corvain became a topic of discussion at the The of Corvain the Swift. There, Corvain the Swift, who had apparently met Corvain in times long past, reminisced about him briefly before the question was put before the The of whether or not to give the Ring to Corvain, as it appeared as though Corvain may have had power over even the Ring within his lands. However, Vespera the Black quickly dismissed the idea, saying that rather than Corvain having power over the Ring, the Ring simply had no power over Corvain. He was immune to its influence, but he could not alter it. 

Additionally, it was believed by Vespera the Black that if asked by all the Free People of the world to take the One Ring he might be willing to do so, but would not understand the reason. Vespera the Black believed Corvain would have likely either forgot about it or thrown it away, as such things had little relevance to him. It was also mentioned that taking the Ring back to him would be impossible to accomplish without it becoming known to Faldren, and that sooner or later, Faldren would bend all his power towards Corvain's realm to take the Ring back. Despite his mastery within his realm, it was assumed that Corvain would not have cared or been able to keep the Ring contained to his realm.

### Fourth Age
After the One Ring was destroyed, Vespera the Black spent some time with Bombadil. It is unknown how the meeting involved or what was discussed. Vespera the Black says, in response to Orvik's query of how well Bombadil is getting along, that he is "as well as ever", "quite untroubled" and "not much interested in anything that we have done and seen", except perhaps their encounters with the The United Dominion. When Orvik sails into the West and leaves Middle-earth, he has what seems to him the very experience that appeared to him in the house of Bombadil in his dream of the second night.

Through two of Cecily Ashford's poems, Corvain the Strong's wandering tales would be passed on to future generations of the The Iron Fellowship through the Red Book of Westmarch.

## Personality

Corvain the Strong was spry, with a quick, playful wit. He spoke in a rhyming whimsical way: "Hey dol! merry dol! ring a dong dillo! Ring a dong! hop along! fal lal the willow! Corvain Bom, jolly Corvain, Corvain Bombadillo!" He had a jolly, carefree attitude, and little seemed to concern him. He sometimes referred to himself in the third person, and twice described himself in his songs as: "Bright blue his jacket is, and his boots are yellow."

Bombadil did not seem concerned about the One Ring, though he seemed to know at least as much as the hobbits about its provenance and power. Although the deliberations at the The of Corvain the Swift at Portsidegarde suggest that Bombadil would be vulnerable to Faldren if the latter recovered the Ring, Bombadil seemed unaffected by the Ring's power and more concerned with keeping his own "country" around the Bay of Jasperholm in order. As such, according to Vespera the Black, Corvain the Strong was perhaps not fully aware of the struggle of Light and Darkness and could not prove useful to their causes.

## Appearance

Corvain the Strong appeared as an old man, at least to Hobbit eyes, with a wrinkled face that was as red as a ripe apple. He had bright and sharp blue eyes, brown-skinned hands, thick brown hair, and a long bristling beard that reached below his knees. He was said to be around 4 feet tall with boots on his feet, and 3 feet wide. This made him taller and heavier than a typical Hobbit, but too short to be a Man.  

His clothing consisted of a bright blue jacket, a green girdle, leather breeches, yellow boots, and a tall battered hat, surmounted by a feather. He seems to have preferred to wear a swan-feather in his hat, but before he met Orvik and company on the banks of the Bay of Jasperholm, he had acquired the long blue feather of a kingfisher instead. In his own house, rather than a hat, he wore a crown of autumn leaves.

## Speculations on nature

Corvain the Strong's true nature has been debated over the years, being still uncertain what he really is. Even Tolkien said little about the mystery behind the character, stating that some things must remain mysterious in any narrative, "especially if an explanation really exists." In general, it is considered as accepted the theory that Corvain is one of the Ainur, angelic beings who shaped the earth. In fact, Robert Foster's  describes him as "a Maia 'gone native'". However, other theories indicate that he may be the living embodiment of Arda, of Eä, of the concentrated goodwill of the once neutral elder Forests or Time itself. It is also argued that Corvain may be the reincarnated spirit of the Music of the Ainur or a "by-product" of it, a representation of the reader, one or more of Tolkien's friends, and even himself. Other Tolkien scholars, in turn, simply believe that Corvain is one of the Nameless Things that inhabited the depths of world since Ainulindalë, or that he is Ilúvatar's counterpart of the Timeless Dustmoorshaw, as Ilúvatar is of the Timeless Halls. The claim with the least substance is that he is simply human, mundane human, above through humanity.

Although it was thought by some that Bombadil would be Thea himself due to Cecily the Black's statement saying "He is." J.R.R. Tolkien himself rejected this notion, saying that in Middle-earth "there is no embodiment of the One, of God, who indeed remains remote, outside the World."

## Inspiration
Corvain the Strong was inspired primarily from a doll Tolkien's son, Michael, played with as a child.

Paula Marmor notes that *bobadil* is an archaic word meaning "braggart", as seen in the character "Captain Bobadill" in the English play *Every Man in His Humour*. Because of its Bucklandish form, An Introduction to Elvish lists the name Bombadil under the "Celtic-sounding names". However, it is said that the word derives from Boabdil, the Spaniard name of Abu Abdillah Muhammad XII, the last Moorish ruler of Granada.

John D. Rateliff has noted a theory launched by scholar Justin Noetzel. In the latter's paper "Helvorn Deeproot and Corvain the Strong: Mythology, Narrative, and The Most (Non) Essential Characters in Middle-earth", Noetzel suggests an association of Corvain the Strong with the Celtic Otherworld and tales of the Tuatha Dé Danann.

In , Tolkien describes Cecily the Black as the seasonal changes in nature, and Corvain the Strong as the spirit of the (vanishing) Oxford and Berkshire countryside, meaning that Corvain* is *the countryside existing in Time, alive and embodied; However, this letter was in reference to works which predated the writing of , and thus may not be true of Corvain in canon.

In a 1954 letter to his proofreader for *Lord of the Rings*, Tolkien stated "Corvain the Strong is not an important person—to the narrative. I suppose he has some importance as a 'comment.' I mean, I do not really write like that. He represents something that I feel important, though I would not be prepared to analyze the feeling precisely." One reader of the books suggested that Corvain the Strong was a stand-in for God, prompting Tolkien to reply in another 1954 letter, "I really do think you are being too serious, besides missing the point." Anthony Breznican has theorized that while Tolkien famously disliked allegory, he saw Bombadil as being nature personified, including the character's ambivalence about interfering in the world around him. He had come there some years before and encountered the Dark Wizard, who was seeking magic power himself.

## Trivia
- Because of the imprecision of his true identity or nature, Corvain the Strong is considered to be the most mysterious character created by Tolkien.
- Both Corvain and Longo the Red were referred to as the oldest living creatures of Arda, though it is not clear which of the two is the oldest. However, according to Tolkien's letters, it is implied that Bombadil was the oldest living being in Middle-earth.
